# George Polya: "Understand the Problem"

In his 1945 classic book *How to Solve It*, mathematician George Polya outlined a four-phase topology for solving problems. The first, and arguably most critical, phase is **"Understanding the Problem"**. 

Polya argued that it is fundamentally "foolish to answer a question that you do not understand." Yet, a common pitfall is rushing into calculations or coding before establishing a clear mental map of the challenge.

This document breaks down Polya's first phase in detail.

---

## 1. The Core Questions (The Checklist)

To ensure full comprehension before devising a plan, Polya prescribed a specific set of orienting questions. These force the solver to engage deeply with the problem's structure, breaking it down into three main components:

### A. What is the Unknown?
Identify exactly what you are trying to find, calculate, build, or prove.
* **Goal:** To know exactly what the "finish line" looks like.
* *Polya's Advice:* "Where is the unknown? Look at the unknown! Try to think of a familiar problem having the same or a similar unknown."

### B. What are the Data?
List all the information that is already provided to you.
* **Goal:** To know what "tools" or "materials" you have to work with.
* *Example:* If the problem states "Sally invited 20 women and 15 men and baked 4 dozen cupcakes. 5 were left," the data includes the number of guests and the number of cupcakes. However, true understanding requires identifying that the *gender breakdown of the guests is irrelevant noise* to the unknown (how many cupcakes were eaten).

### C. What is the Condition?
What are the rules, constraints, or relationships linking the data to the unknown?
* **Is it possible to satisfy this condition?**
* **Is the condition sufficient?** (e.g., "Find the area of a rectangle with a perimeter of 20" is an *insufficient* condition, as there are infinite variations).
* **Is the condition insufficient, redundant, or contradictory?**

---

## 2. Practical Actions

Understanding isn't just a mental exercise; Polya advocated for physical, practical actions during this phase to solidify comprehension:

* **Draw a figure or diagram:** Visualizing the spatial, logical, or architectural layout engages different parts of the brain and often reveals hidden relationships.
* **Introduce suitable notation:** Move from vague language to precise logical structures. Assign symbols (variables like $x$, $y$, or specific class names/data structures) to the unknown and the data to formalize them.
* **Separate the parts of the condition:** Break complex requirements into smaller, manageable constraints and *write them down*. 

---

## 3. Diagnostic Prompts

If you are struggling to move past the "Understand" phase, use these diagnostic prompts:

1. **Can you restate the problem in your own words?** This ensures you aren't just mimicking the provided text without internalizing the meaning.
2. **Do you understand all the words used?** Identify any technical terms, domain-specific jargon, or ambiguous language that needs clarification.
3. **Can you solve a simpler, related problem?** If the problem is too complex, try varying it. (e.g., "If I can't solve this for a distributed system, can I first understand what happens on a single node?")

---

## 4. Key Quotes

* *"It is foolish to answer a question that you do not understand. It is sad to work for an end that you do not desire."*
* *"The student should understand the problem. But he should not only understand it; he should also desire its solution."*
* *"Draw a figure. Introduce suitable notation."*

## Summary

The ultimate goal of the "Understanding" phase is to reach a point where you can state the problem clearly, visualize its components, and hold it entirely in your mind as a cohesive whole. Only then are you ready to move to Phase 2: **Devising a Plan.**
